# LABEL EXAMPLES — 3 different vibes

---

## Example 1: Cold Meat Industry (Label, P1 — Dark Ambient / Death Industrial cathedral)

Cold Meat Industry was the institution. Roger Karmanik founded the label in Mjölby, Sweden, in 1987, and over the next twenty-five years built a catalog that defined — and for many listeners, constituted — the dark ambient and death industrial genres. The CMI roster reads like a syllabus: Raison d'Etre, Deutsch Nepal, Brighter Death Now, MZ.412, In Slaughter Natives, Desiderii Marginis, Atrium Carceri. Each distinct. All unmistakably CMI.

The label's aesthetic was total: artwork uniformly dark and ritualistic, releases mastered to a specific sonic density, catalog numbers sequential and comprehensive. Karmanik's own projects — Brighter Death Now, Lille Roger — operated within the same framework, the label owner as practitioner rather than executive. Format emphasis shifted from cassette in the early years to CD through the 1990s and 2000s, with vinyl reserved for select releases.

CMI closed in 2012 after 25 years of operation. Karmanik continued through Cold Spring Records, but the CMI catalog remains the single most important body of work in Scandinavian dark ambient — a closed archive whose influence only grows with distance.

## Example 2: Tesco Organisation (Label, P1 — Power Electronics, uncompromising)

Tesco Organisation doesn't curate. It selects — and the selection criteria have remained consistent since its founding in 1986: uncompromising power electronics, death industrial, and noise from artists who mean it. The German label, operated from Munich, built a roster that reads like a threat assessment: Genocide Organ, Anenzephalia, Brighter Death Now, Con-Dom, Atrax Morgue.

The releases are uniformly austere: minimal packaging, no decoration beyond the necessary, catalog numbers that function as identifiers rather than marketing. Tesco's role in the power electronics scene is structural — it provides infrastructure for work that no other label would touch, not because it's commercially unviable (though it is) but because it's genuinely hostile. The label doesn't explain, contextualize, or apologize for its roster. It releases the work and moves on.

## Example 3: Staalplaat (Label, P1 — Eclectic experimental, curatorial range)

Geert-Jan Hobijn founded Staalplaat in Amsterdam in 1983, and for two decades the label functioned as one of experimental music's most adventurous curatorial platforms. The roster was deliberately incoherent: Hafler Trio next to Kapotte Muziek next to Merzbow next to PBK next to Organum. What connected the catalog was not genre but a shared commitment to sound as primary medium — every release, regardless of style, treated recorded sound as material to be shaped rather than a vehicle for composition.

Staalplaat's physical editions were themselves objects: hand-assembled packaging, unusual formats (the Mort aux Vaches series of live recordings, the Staaltape cassette series), and the Staalplaat Soundsystem installations that extended the label's practice into gallery and museum contexts. The operation combined Dutch pragmatism with genuine curatorial ambition — running a record shop, a distribution network, and a label simultaneously, all from the same Amsterdam address.
