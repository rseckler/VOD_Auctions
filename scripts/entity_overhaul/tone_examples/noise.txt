## Example 1: Merzbow (Artist, P1)

Merzbow is Masami Akita, and Masami Akita has been generating noise since 1979. Not experimenting with it. Not exploring it. Generating it — as a continuous, unrelenting output that now spans over 400 releases across every conceivable format. The project is named after Kurt Schwitters' Merzbau, but where Schwitters accumulated found objects into sculpture, Akita accumulates feedback, distortion, and overdriven electronics into walls of sound so dense they function as physical phenomena rather than recordings.

The early work — cassettes and LPs on ZSF Produkt, Lowest Music & Arts, Old Europa Cafe — established the methodology: layer upon layer of distortion until individual sounds cease to exist and what remains is a single tectonic mass of noise. Pulse Demon (1996, Release Entertainment) became the unlikely entry point for a generation of listeners, its sheer aggression paradoxically accessible through its consistency — seventy minutes of unbroken saturation that either repels in the first thirty seconds or absorbs completely.

Akita's range is wider than the "Japanese noise" label suggests. Projects with Balázs Pándi (drums), collaborations with Boris, excursions into harsh noise wall, junk metal percussion, laptop-processed animal recordings, and a prolific run of releases exploring bird-related themes across the 2010s demonstrate an artist for whom noise is not a genre but a medium — as varied and inexhaustible as paint.

MEMBERS:
- Masami Akita — electronics, tapes, junk metal, concept (1979–present)

## Example 2: Incapacitants (Artist, P2)

Toshiji Mikawa and Fumio Kosakai have been operating as Incapacitants since 1981, producing noise that treats subtlety as an obstacle to be destroyed. The Tokyo duo's approach is maximalist in the purest sense: two performers generating feedback and distortion simultaneously, each escalating against the other until the combined output exceeds what recording equipment was designed to capture. Their live performances — sweat-soaked, physically demanding, equipment-destroying — are the project's primary medium. The recordings are evidence that it happened.

MEMBERS:
- Toshiji Mikawa — electronics, feedback (1981–present)
- Fumio Kosakai — electronics, feedback (1981–present)

## Example 3: Hijokaidan (Artist, P2)

Jojo Hiroshige founded Hijokaidan in Osaka in 1979, making it one of the earliest Japanese noise projects — predating even Merzbow's first recordings. The name means "emergency stairway," which functions as an accurate description of the experience: a narrow, disorienting path through escalating chaos. Hiroshige's guitar, fed through chains of distortion pedals and amplifiers, produces a sound that has less in common with rock music than with natural disasters. The rotating cast of collaborators — at times including Junko (vocals) whose screamed performances are themselves legendary — ensures that no two performances are alike, though all share the same commitment to total sonic overload.

MEMBERS:
- Jojo Hiroshige — guitar, electronics (1979–present), founder
- Junko — vocals (various periods)
