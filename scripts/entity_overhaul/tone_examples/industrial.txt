# TONE: industrial

## Example 1: Einstürzende Neubauten (Artist, P1)

Einstürzende Neubauten — "Collapsing New Buildings" — took the name literally. Blixa Bargeld (vocals, guitar), N.U. Unruh (percussion, metal), Alexander Hacke (bass, guitar), and Mark Chung (bass) formed the group in West Berlin in 1980 and proceeded to build instruments from construction site debris, power tools, and scrap metal. They didn't sample industrial sound. They made it.

The early recordings — Kollaps (1981), Zeichnungen des Patienten O.T. (1983) — are documentation of physical labor as much as music: sheet metal beaten with hammers, pneumatic drills played rhythmically, steel springs bowed with metal rods. Bargeld's vocals range from guttural screaming to whispered German poetry, always positioned as one more texture in the machinery rather than its centerpiece.

Their trajectory from demolition-site noise to composed, even delicate arrangements by the 2000s (Perpetuum Mobile, Alles Wieder Offen) traces the arc of a group that spent decades learning to build after years of tearing down. The self-funded Supporter Project, launched in 2002, pioneered the direct-to-fan model a decade before Kickstarter existed.

MEMBERS:
- Blixa Bargeld — vocals, guitar, concept (1980–present), founder
- N.U. Unruh — custom percussion, metal (1980–present), founder
- Alexander Hacke — bass, guitar, electronics (1980–present)
- Mark Chung — bass (1980–1994)
- Jochen Arbeit — guitar (1997–present)
- Rudolf Moser — custom percussion (1997–present)

## Example 2: Test Dept (Artist, P2)

Test Dept emerged from New Cross, South London, in 1981 with a sound built from scrap. The collective — at various points numbering up to a dozen members — performed on oil drums, metal plate, angle grinders, and industrial detritus salvaged from the derelict docks of the Thames. Their early cassettes and the debut LP Beating the Retreat (1984, Some Bizzare) document a group for whom percussion was ideology: working-class bodies producing music from the refuse of the industries that employed and discarded them.

Their politics were overt — benefit concerts for the miners' strike, collaborations with the South Wales Striking Miners Choir, soundtracks for agitprop film. The sound was functional: metronomic, exhausting, and deliberately unglamorous. Industrial music as actual labor.

## Example 3: Die Krupps (Artist, P2)

Jürgen Engler and Bernward Malaka launched Die Krupps in Düsseldorf in 1981, naming the project after the Krupp steel dynasty — a statement of intent that proved accurate. The early work, particularly Stahlwerksinfonie (1981), was built from hammered metal percussion and minimal electronics: the sound of the Ruhr Valley rendered as music. Their evolution through EBM (Entering the Arena, 1985) and eventually industrial metal in the 1990s followed a logical trajectory — each phase adding propulsion while retaining the mechanical discipline of the original concept.

MEMBERS:
- Jürgen Engler — vocals, electronics (1981–present), founder
- Bernward Malaka — bass, electronics (1981–present), founder
