## Example 1: Whitehouse (Artist, P1)

Whitehouse didn't invite you to listen. William Bennett founded the project in 1980 in London with a single premise: total sonic aggression. No melody. No rhythm. No compromise. Just high-frequency oscillators driven into distortion, feedback locked at pain threshold, and Bennett's vocals — not sung, not spoken, but delivered like verbal assault.

The early recordings on Bennett's own Come Organisation — Birthdeath Experience (1980), Dedicated to Peter Kürten (1981), Buchenwald (1981) — established power electronics as a genre by refusing to be anything else. Where Throbbing Gristle provoked through ambiguity, Whitehouse provoked through directness. The subject matter — serial killers, sexual violence, sadism — was presented without commentary, context, or apology.

Bennett's collaborators across the project's lifespan included Philip Best (electronics, vocals), Peter Sotos (text, concept), Kevin Tomkins (electronics), and Glenn Michael Wallis. Each brought different intensities, but the project's identity remained Bennett's: relentless, confrontational, and indifferent to whether anyone was still in the room.

After 2008, Bennett retired the Whitehouse name in favor of Cut Hands, pivoting toward Afro-Caribbean percussion rhythms. The pivot was total. The old work remains — undiluted.

MEMBERS:
- William Bennett — electronics, vocals, concept (1980–2008), founder
- Philip Best — electronics, vocals (1982–2008)
- Peter Sotos — text, concept (1982–1985)
- Kevin Tomkins — electronics (1980s)
- Glenn Michael Wallis — electronics (1980s)

## Example 2: Con-Dom (Artist, P2)

Con-Dom is Mike Dando. One man. One microphone. No backing. Since 1984, Dando has operated from the English Midlands producing some of the most abrasive and uncompromising power electronics in the genre's history. His method is stripped to essentials: distorted vocals, feedback manipulation, and an unwavering commitment to confrontation that has never softened across four decades of releases on Tesco Organisation, Cold Meat Industry, and his own label.

Where other PE artists layer noise into walls of sound, Dando leaves space — raw, exposed space that makes the next blast of feedback hit harder. His live performances are exercises in endurance for both performer and audience.

MEMBERS:
- Mike Dando — vocals, electronics (1984–present)

## Example 3: Prurient (Artist, P2)

Dominick Fernow built Prurient as a vehicle for catharsis through volume. Active since the late 1990s from New York, the project exists at the intersection of power electronics, harsh noise, and something that occasionally resembles black metal stripped of its riffs. Fernow's screamed vocals cut through walls of distorted synthesizer — not performative aggression but something that sounds involuntary, like sound being extracted under pressure.

His prolific output across Hospital Productions (his own label), Load Records, and Hydra Head ranges from the sheet-metal abrasion of early cassettes to the more structured synthwave-influenced work on Frozen Niagara Falls (2015, Profound Lore). The scope shifts. The intensity does not.

MEMBERS:
- Dominick Fernow — vocals, electronics, synthesizers (1997–present)
