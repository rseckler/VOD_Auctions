## Example 1: Nurse With Wound (Artist, P1)

Steven Stapleton has operated Nurse With Wound since 1978 as a vehicle for whatever interests him at any given moment — and those interests have included musique concrete, surrealist collage, free improvisation, hallucinatory pop, drone, and extended exercises in bewilderment. The debut album Chance Meeting on a Dissecting Table of a Sewing Machine and an Umbrella (1979, United Dairies) — titled after Lautreamont, sounding like nothing in particular — arrived with an insert listing 291 artists Stapleton considered influences. That list became known as "The NWW List" and remains one of the most referenced documents in experimental music.

The catalog sprawls across over 100 releases and defies summary because it defies consistency. Homotopy to Marie (1982) is abstract tape composition. Thunder Perfect Mind (1992) is hypnotic, almost meditative drone. Salt Marie Celeste (2003) is beatific ambient. Huffin Rag Blues (2008) is improvised absurdism. The connecting thread is Stapleton's curatorial intelligence — every project, however disparate, shares a surrealist logic where juxtaposition is the primary compositional method.

Stapleton's visual art — collage, painting, sculpture — is inseparable from the music. Album covers are his own work, and the aesthetic of visual and sonic absurdity forms a unified practice rather than parallel activities.

MEMBERS:
- Steven Stapleton — everything, concept (1978–present), founder
- Colin Potter — electronics, engineering (frequent collaborator since 1980s)
- Andrew Liles — electronics (frequent collaborator since 1990s)

## Example 2: Organum (Artist, P2)

David Jackman's Organum has pursued a single question since 1983: what happens when you remove everything from music except resonance? The answer, across dozens of releases on United Dairies, Die Stadt, and his own Aeroplane, is a body of work that exists at the boundary between composition and acoustic phenomena — bowed metal, struck bells, organ tones sustained until they decompose into overtones, silence treated as structural material rather than absence.

Jackman's method is reductive in the most literal sense: each piece strips away until what remains is not melody or rhythm but the physical behavior of vibrating matter. The recordings demand patience and repay it with an altered sense of how sound occupies time.

MEMBERS:
- David Jackman — metal, bells, organ, concept (1983–present)

## Example 3: Hafler Trio (Artist, P2)

Andrew McKenzie launched the Hafler Trio (stylized h3o) in 1983 as a conceptual project that treats recorded sound as epistemology — not what does this sound like, but what does listening do to the listener? The output, primarily on Touch, Staalplaat, and his own label, ranges from processed field recordings to near-silence to dense electronic manipulation, always accompanied by liner notes and theoretical frameworks drawn from psychoacoustics, Korzybski's general semantics, and Sufi philosophy.

Whether the conceptual apparatus is essential or decorative is a question McKenzie would likely argue proves his point. The recordings themselves reward attention regardless of framework: carefully constructed sound environments that shift subtly on repeated listening, revealing structure where first exposure found only texture.

MEMBERS:
- Andrew McKenzie — concept, electronics, text (1983–present), founder
