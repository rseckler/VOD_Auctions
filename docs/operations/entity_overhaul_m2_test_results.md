## Throbbing Gristle

**Typ:** artist · **Releases:** 325 · **Tier:** obscure · **Tone:** `industrial` · **ID:** `legacy-artist-1892`

### Beschreibung

_255 Wörter_

Throbbing Gristle dissolved in 1995. The twenty-year experiment in calculated provocation left behind 325 catalogued items — magazines, cassettes, vinyl, photographs — and a fundamental redefinition of what music could be.

Genesis P-Orridge and Cosey Fanni Tutti formed the group in Kingston upon Hull in 1975, evolving from the performance art entity COUM Transmissions. Peter Christopherson and Chris Carter joined later. Their first public appearance came at the COUM exhibition Prostitution in October 1976 — a deliberately offensive gesture positioned as critique of the art establishment. They established Industrial Records the same year. The label's name was declarative: industrial as the material, not the genre.

The early work — The Second Annual Report (1977), United/Zyklon B Zombie (1978) — assembled from tape collages, found sound, and primitive electronics. The methodology mattered as much as the output. They recorded police interviews, surveillance footage, industrial noise, and manipulated it into compositions that refused the category of music. The 1980 album Heathen Earth captured live sessions at the Death Factory: bare, confrontational, engineered for discomfort.

By 1983's 20 Jazz Funk Greats — a title itself a provocation — the group had incorporated more structured arrangements while maintaining the clinical edge. The catalog diversified: Nothing Special (1984), the live document Sacrifice (1986), the compilation Once Upon A Time (1990). The format distribution in this archive tells its own story: 152 magazines, 76 cassettes, 41 LPs. A paper trail as extensive as the sonic one.

They ended in 1995. Genesis P-Orridge continued the work through other projects. The framework remained.

### Beschreibung

_245 Wörter_

Psychic TV emerged in 1981, birthed directly from the collapse of Throbbing Gristle. When Genesis P-Orridge and Alex Fergusson formed the collective, they carried forward the industrial methodology: every medium was material, every performance was documentation, every release was an artifact. The name promised transcendence. The sound delivered something more visceral.

The early work — cassettes, vinyl, video — occupied the same brutalist territory as its predecessor. Peter Christopherson, John Balance, and P-Orridge built ritualistic noise structures: cyclical, hypnotic, deliberately trance-inducing. They weren't interested in composition as convention. They were interested in state change. The body as instrument. The audience as participant. The performance as ceremony.

By the late 1980s into the 1990s, the output became staggering. Towards Thee Infinite Beat (1990) merged industrial repetition with psychedelic pulse — a bridge between the underground and the warehouse raves consuming British youth culture. Re-Mind (1993) and Stations Ov Thee Cross (1994) continued this synthesis: acid house rhythms refracted through ritual electronics. The cassette catalog alone — 68 entries in this archive — represents a documentation practice as relentless as the music itself.

The 224 releases spanning magazines, cassettes, LPs, and video reflect not prolificness but compulsion. Each format was a different channel for the same inquiry: how sound and image could restructure consciousness. The Temple of Psychic Youth (TOPY) formalized this as ideology — a network, a publication, a doctrine.

This was not psychedelic escape. This was industrial machinery working toward other states. Functional. Relentless. Transgressive.

### Beschreibung

_292 Wörter_

By 1973, the Bay Area had already begun generating its own experimental music ecosystem — loose, geographically dispersed, connected through mail order catalogs and word of mouth. What it lacked was a printed nervous system. EAR Magazine became that system.

Published monthly from San Francisco, the magazine traced an 18-year arc through the heart of American new music, documenting what happened when post-Cage avant-garde practice collided with emerging feminist theory, environmental consciousness, and radical political organizing. This wasn't incidental coverage — the editorial lens treated these intersections as inseparable from the music itself.EAR Volume 15 Number 04 June 1990, for instance, arrives stacked with concert announcements, critical essays, and correspondence that treats the experimental community as a functioning organism rather than a collection of isolated artists.

The event listings alone made the publication indispensable. Each issue carried pages of upcoming performances across the Bay Area — warehouse shows, university concerts, gallery interventions — functioning as the underground's scheduling infrastructure before digital alternatives existed. As the magazine matured and shifted focus toward New York in its later years, these listings expanded to map a national network of new music activity.

What distinguished EAR from other music press of the period was its refusal to position experimental music as a genre requiring translation for mainstream audiences. The publication assumed its readers belonged to the scene. There were no primers. Instead, the magazine operated as an internal bulletin — dense, partisan, deeply embedded in the communities it covered. The 1991 issues — Volume 15 Number 09 January 1991 through Volume 16 Number 02 May 1991 — represent the final dispatches from a publication that had spent nearly two decades insisting that experimental music was not separate from political and social life, but continuous with it.
